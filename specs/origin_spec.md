**Rhythmic Attunement to the Heart**

This app is developed for people with atrial fibrillation.

A preliminary step:

This app will work with a user name and a password as in most apps.

The first time the user uses this app he will need to define what is a normal heart rate for him and what is a too fast heart rate. The default for a normal heart rate will be 70 bpm. The default for too fast heartrate will be 100 bpm.

The user will also choose 3 types of music that he likes to listen to - it will offer common genres like pop, classical music, etc.

The user can change all these definitions in a friendly menu in the app, whenever he wants to.

Whenever the app is open the user can see his heartrate. The number of his heart bpm will be on the screen in the corner, all the time.

First step:

The app gets input from a watch or other device that can measure the user's heart rate, and it gets a notification from the device once the heart rate measured is faster than the too fast heartrate as defined by the user in the app.

Then the app will open and suggest the user - It's time to give your heart a break.

And ask - Would you like to open the app?

If he says "no" - The app respond - ok, just be aware - your heart is over a 100 bpm, see you next time. And the app will close.

If the patient says "yes" the app opens with a welcome page with "Hello (User's name)".

Second step - synchronization:

The app should have a very inviting look. With a calming effect. It should be easy to operate just like YouTube music or Spotify.

It should learn the musical taste of the user based on his preferences and choices and keep learning it every time the user uses the app.

Once the session starts the user's music should be according to his heartbeat.

For instance, If the input from the user's heartrate device says 150 bpm, then the music that he will listen to should be around 150 bpm. Most important it will not suggest music in a faster tempo. And it will not suggest music in a much slower tempo. The tempo of the music will be maximum like the input, in this case 150 bpm, and minimum 5% bpm slower, in this case 143. In case like this were the 5% slower is not an integer, like 142.5, it will round the number up to the closest integer, like in this example to 143.

Which means the user will listen to music between 143-150 bpm in this example.

In order for that to happen, this app should be able to take every song it gets and calculate its bpm. It should be able to know before the song is even offered for the user if this song is good for him in the state that he is in. and offer only music that can synch with his heart.

In order to have a wide variety of songs and musical pieces, the app should be able to take every song it gets and change its bpm a little bit. It should be able to play every song between 5% bpm slower than the normal bpm and 5% bpm faster than the normal bpm.

After 2 minutes or when the song ends (whichever comes first) the app checks with the device again to see what is the heart rate.

If the heart went to a faster speed, the music will try to adapt. If the increase is less than 5% it will make the music faster to the correct bpm. So, if the user's bpm change slightly in this range of plus or minus 5% bpm it will not change the song, but it will adapt the music the user's heartbeat.

But if the difference is more than 5% it will choose a faster tempo music again between 5% bpm slower than the heartbeat and maximum, the heartbeat.

Once the heartrate is synched with the music bpm there will pop a massege on the screen saying to the user that a synchronization happened now let's try to slow down.

Step 3 - after synchronizing, try to slow the heart down:

After the music synchronizes with the user's heart rate, it tries to slow the heart down. It does it by choosing a music a bit slower than the heart rate.

It can offer music between 10% bpm to 5% bpm slower than the heartbeat. If 90% bpm is not a round integer it will round up to the closest integer. If the 95% bpm is not a rounded integer it will round down to the closest integer.

According to that, in the example above, where the user's bpm is 150, it will offer music between 135-142 bpm.

Step 4 - continue the treatment -

every 2 minutes, or when the song ends (whichever comes first), the app will check the heart rate (it checks it from the input device).

If it changed less than 5% it adapts the current music.

If it changed more than 5% it changes the music to a different song.

If a synchronization accurses and the heartbeat of the user slowed down, it goes back to step 3 and tries to slow down the user's heartbeat even more.

Every time the heartrate slows down by 10% bpm, the user gets a notification with a popup message on the screen that says: Great Job! Your heart slowed down by 10%, let's continue calming down.

Step 5 - The heart goes back to a normal rhythm

Once the heartrate measured by the device gets to the normal heartrate as defined by the user, the App gives a notification: "Well done. Your heartrate is back to normal".

It will ask the user if he wants to continue listening to calming music or exit the app.

If he says he wants to continue listening, then the app will continue playing music with maximum bpm as defined by the user as normal heartrate and in any case, not more than 80 bpm.

If he says he want to exit, then the app closes.
